“BYJU’S style of videos” means a very specific edtech explainer format:

# High-energy teacher-led explanation
A presenter or voiceover explains concepts in a confident, classroom-like but polished tone. It feels less like a lecture and more like a guided “let me show you how this works” session.

# Strong visual storytelling
Concepts are not just written on screen. They are shown through diagrams, animations, objects, characters, real-world examples, and metaphors.

# Clean digital-board / animated-board aesthetic
Often a dark or colorful background, with bright text, arrows, labels, hand-drawn-style highlights, zoom-ins, and step-by-step build-up of information.

# Concept-first, not textbook-first
Instead of saying “definition → formula → example,” the video usually starts with curiosity: a question, real-life situation, misconception, or visual hook.

# Contextual motion and reveals
Text and graphics enter gradually. Diagrams build piece by piece. Important words pop, circle, underline, glow, or move.

# Teacher + animation combo
Either the teacher appears on screen beside animations, or the entire video is animated with a strong instructor voice guiding it.

# Simplified but curiosoty igniting explanations
Even simple ideas are made to feel exciting: “Imagine this…”, “What if…?”, “Let’s zoom inside…”, “Here’s the trick…”

# Exam-learning orientation
The content is designed to help students understand and remember quickly, often with shortcuts, recap moments, and question-solving.

# Interpret BYJU’S style as:

A polished, animated explainer video where a concept is taught through visual hooks, step-by-step animated diagrams, energetic narration, and real-world examples — more cinematic than a normal classroom lecture, but still academically structured.

Let me give you a brief of how we built the BYJU'S videos. I, Priyadarshi Siddharth, Ex-VP at BYJU'S, was one of the pioneers building and innovating how we created the videos for a decade a there.

All maths notation moves happened, manually step-by-step in After Effects with every element that needed movement or highlight, in a separate layer, the classic after effects style.

All diagram geometric patterns, labelling, angle marks, figure being constructed callouts happened using After Effects.

All the layouts title cards, comparison, teacher on the side, et cetera happen using manual storyboard, using the most intuitive  instructional design things that we found work best with us.

This thing started as a very organic team, which was very creatively enthusiastic about  creating everything. Instead of creating one design template and running the entire production on it, the team was not satisfied with that and naively (as for most it was their 1st job) for the best, they chose creating new set of templates for every chapter in After Effects; hapter of textbook I mean. Gradually, we extended this philosophy to creating contextual templates, we mean if the chapter is on atom or sound, the visual looking feel of  the motion templates will resemble something which has intrinsic viusal qualities of atoms or sound waves inatead of just a random looking good design. Later we learnt better and instead of every chapter having one template, we created every topic theme having similar template (still not same :p; kept color variation as minimum change).

So even the templates were not mechanical, they were unique to every chapter in visual and design. For example, the way everything will come in sound chapter will have a lot of wave like motion. In a chapter on atoms, it will have basic model of an atom sqishing in and going out and revealing elements, et cetera.

So most of the things ran on human creativity and enthusism to outdo the last creative work.

The magic actually was:

- Wedeliberately rejected the one-master-template model. Each chapter — later each topic theme — got its own visual and motion language, and that language was semantically tied to the concept: wave motion for sound, an atom model squishing in to reveal elements. The motion itself carried meaning. Color was the minimum within-theme variation; across themes, fresh design.

- That contextual inventiveness was the product, driven by a team competing to outdo the last piece.

- The mechanical part was the After Effects grind: every moving/highlighted element on its own layer, hand-keyframed step-by-step, synced to the VO.
